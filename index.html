Roman Pinsa

The ancient Roman flatbread with a light, airy texture and crispy crust that's taking the culinary world by storm

The History of Pinsa

The term "pinsa" derives from the Latin word pinsere, meaning "to stretch" or "press out," referring to the traditional method of hand-stretching the dough into its characteristic oval shape.

Dating back over 2000 years to ancient Rome, pinsa was originally a simple flatbread made by farmers using a mixture of cereals like millet, barley, and spelt. It was considered poor man's food, often topped simply with olive oil, salt, and aromatic herbs.

The modern version of Pinsa Romana was refined around 2001 by Corrado Di Marco, who developed the special flour blend that gives pinsa its distinctive light, airy texture and high digestibility. Since then, pinsa has experienced a culinary renaissance with thousands of specialized shops (pinserie) opening throughout Italy and worldwide.

Pinsa vs. Pizza: The Differences

  • Uses a blend of wheat, rice, and soy/spelt flours
  • Higher hydration (80% water content)
  • Oval shape rather than round
  • Longer fermentation (24-72 hours)
  • Lighter, more digestible, and airier texture
  • Crispy exterior with soft interior

Authentic Roman Pinsa Recipe

Dough Ingredients

Makes 2-3 medium-sized pinsa

  • 500g Italian "00" flour (or all-purpose flour)
  • 50g rice flour (essential for authentic texture)
  • 40g spelt flour (or soy flour as alternative)
  • 460ml ice-cold water (about 80% hydration)
  • 2.5g active dry yeast (about ¾ tsp)
  • 10g salt (about 1¾ tsp)
  • 5ml extra virgin olive oil (1 tsp), plus more for topping
  • Semolina flour for dusting and shaping

Equipment Needed:

  • Large mixing bowl
  • Stand mixer with dough hook (optional but helpful)
  • Pizza stone or heavy baking sheet
  • Kitchen scissors (best for cutting without crushing air pockets)

Step-by-Step Instructions

This process spans 2-3 days, but the actual hands-on time is minimal. The magic happens during the long fermentation.

Day 1: Prepare the Dough

  1. 1

    Mix dry ingredients

    In a large bowl, whisk together the "00" flour, rice flour, and spelt flour with the yeast until well combined.

  2. 2

    Add water gradually

    Add about ¾ of the cold water (350ml) to the flour mixture and begin mixing either by hand or with a stand mixer using the dough hook attachment. The dough may look dry at first.

  3. 3

    Add oil and salt

    Add the remaining water with the salt and olive oil. Knead for about 10-12 minutes until the dough becomes smooth and elastic. Note: The dough will be very sticky - resist the urge to add more flour.

  4. 4

    First rest

    Cover the bowl with plastic wrap or a damp towel and let it rest at room temperature for about 1 hour until it doubles in size.

  5. 5

    Fold and refrigerate

    After the initial rise, use a spatula to fold the dough over itself a few times. Place the dough in a clean, lightly oiled bowl, cover tightly, and refrigerate for 24-72 hours. This long, cold fermentation is key to developing flavor and improving digestibility.

Expert Tips:

  • Use ice-cold water to slow fermentation and develop flavor.

  • The dough will be very sticky - use wet or oiled hands to handle it rather than adding more flour.

  • For best flavor development, aim for at least 36 hours of cold fermentation.

Day 2 or 3: Shape and Bake

  1. 1

    Remove from refrigerator

    Take the dough out of the fridge and divide it into 2-3 equal portions, depending on your desired size.

  2. 2

    Second rise

    Form each portion into a ball, place in separate bowls, cover with a cloth, and let rise at room temperature for 2-3 hours until doubled in size.

  3. 3

    Preheat oven

    Heat your oven to its highest setting (preferably 250°C/480°F or higher) with a pizza stone or heavy baking sheet inside.

  4. 4

    Shape the pinsa

    Generously dust your work surface with rice flour. Gently press and stretch the dough with your fingertips into an oval shape about 25-30cm (10-12 inches) long. The dough should be about 1cm thick with slightly thicker edges.

  5. 5

    Transfer and top

    Transfer the shaped dough to a pizza peel or parchment paper sprinkled with semolina flour. Add your desired toppings, or for a simple traditional pinsa, just drizzle with olive oil and sprinkle with sea salt and fresh rosemary.

  6. 6

    Bake

    Slide the pinsa onto the preheated stone or baking sheet and bake for 10-15 minutes until the crust is golden and crispy. Use kitchen scissors to cut without crushing the airy structure.

Shaping Tips:

  • Use your fingertips to dimple the dough rather than flattening it, preserving air bubbles.

  • Be generous with rice flour when shaping to prevent sticking and create the authentic crispy exterior.

  • Let gravity help by gently stretching the dough while holding it up.

Baking Techniques:

  • For wet toppings like tomato sauce, consider baking the base for 5-7 minutes first, then adding toppings and finishing baking.

  • Some toppings like fresh burrata should be added after baking.

  • Don't overload with toppings, which can make the pinsa soggy.

Chef's Pro Tips

Temperature Control

Cold water and cold fermentation are crucial for developing flavor and texture. The slow, controlled process breaks down gluten making the pinsa more digestible and developing complex flavors.

Gentle Handling

The high hydration dough is delicate. Handle with wet or oiled hands, never force shaping, and preserve air bubbles by using fingertips to dimple rather than pressing flat.

Balance in Toppings

Less is more with pinsa toppings. The light, airy crust deserves to shine. Balance flavors by combining sweet, salty, and aromatic elements, and add delicate ingredients after baking.

Troubleshooting Guide

Problems & Solutions

Dough too sticky to handle

Use wet hands or oil them lightly rather than adding more flour. Chill the dough if needed to make it more manageable.

Dough not rising properly

Check yeast freshness. Ensure your room is warm enough during the second rise (around 75°F/24°C is ideal).

Crust not crispy enough

Ensure oven is fully preheated to maximum temperature. Use a pizza stone if possible. Be generous with rice flour when shaping.

Soggy middle

Reduce wet toppings or par-bake the base first. Ensure dough is evenly stretched without thick spots.

Adapting to Your Kitchen

No pizza stone available

Use an inverted heavy baking sheet or cast iron pan, preheated for at least 30 minutes.

No stand mixer

Mix by hand with a wooden spoon, then use the "stretch and fold" technique every 15 minutes for an hour instead of traditional kneading.

Can't find specific flours

Rice flour is essential but can be made by grinding rice in a blender. For spelt/soy, substitute whole wheat flour with a bit more hydration.

Short on time

While 24+ hours is ideal, even 8-12 hours in the refrigerator will give improved texture and flavor over immediate baking.
